The Shawshank Redemption (1994) is widely regarded as one of the most spiritually resonant
cinematic texts of the late twentieth century .2 Its emphasis on spiritual resistance to injustice has very
likely contributed to its enduring position at the top of IMDb’s global rankings . Adapted from Stephen
King’s novella “Rita Hayworth and Shawshank Redemption” (1982), the film belongs to a period i n
King’s writing defined less by supernatural horror than by examinations of injustice, masculinity, and
institutional power. Set in mid -century America, Shawshank occupies the cultural landscape between
postwar optimism and the civil -rights era, a moment when the authority of institutions , law
enforcement, prisons, and the state , was both idealized and increasingly suspect in the aftermath of the
Kennedy assassination, the rumblings of the Vietnam war and political turmoil preceding Watergate .
The film’s w orld is therefore historically grounded in a time when the promise of American justice
was rhetorically celebrated but structurally fragile. Viewers and scholars alike often interpret the story
of Andy Dufresne as a contemporary Christ narrative , which is largely justified : he suffers unjustly,
maintains hope in the face of despair, liberates others, and ultimately rises beyond the walls that
contain him.3 Other readings turn to Foucauldian frameworks, interpreting the prison as a disciplinary
heterotopia, a spatial inverse of society.4 While critics often read Shawshank through biblical typology
or Foucauldian punishment,5 few have examined the film through an explicitly Kierkegaardian lens.
Existing scholarship in religion and film tends to foreground the story’s Christ symbolism, but rarely
its existential theology, its treatment of inwardness, or its pronouncedly postsec ular understanding of
redemption. These existing accounts are compelling and substantial , but they remain largely tethered
to a binary of confinement and freedom that the film quietly subverts in many ways . In Shawshank ,
prison is much more than simply the site of oppression. It is also a refuge from something more
fundamental and perhaps more frightening than punishment: existential anxiety. Danish philosopher
Søren Kierkegaard defines anxiety as “the dizziness of freedom,” the vertigo that arises when a h uman

Human Oscillations: Hope, Despair, Anxiety
Kierkegaard draws a crucial distinction between fear and anxiety. Fear always has an object: it is
directed toward something recognizable in the world. A person fears violence, punishment,
humiliation, or death. Anxiety, by contrast, has no object. It arises not from what is, but from all that
might be. “Anxiety is the dizziness of freedom,” Kierkegaard writes, “the individual’s openness to
possibility.”11 Anxiety appears when the self confronts the realization that it alone must choose its life,
and that there is no external guarantee that these choices are secure, meaningful, or right —suggesting
an exi stential conundrum that verges on the pathological in modern societies. Rightly so, viewers
of The Shawshank Redemption experience fear for Andy : toward the Sisters, the guards, the warden,
the threat of solitary confinement. They themselves may also fear the idea of getting caught in the
confines of punitive caprice. But the prisoners themselves live in anxiety: the vertigo of possibility
that returns the moment they imagine freedom. Of importance to the film’s universalist message,
Kierkegaa rd himself describes anxiety not as a pathological disturbance but a structural feature of
human existence. Where Foucauldian readings rely on the recognition that a prison narrative points
the audience towards their own confined state in the wider rule -work of society, a Kierkegaardian
perspective on the film shows us anxiety in its most condensed form. In The Concept of Anxiety , he

humankind inherits not only Adam’s guilt, but Adam’s condition.13 Accordingly , anxiety is not a
punishment but the deepest expression of what it means to be human: the burden of possibility. Anxiety
and despair are therefore inseparable. In The Sickness Unto Death , Kierkegaard goes on to define
despair not as punctuated dramatic suf fering, but as a failure of the self to relate properly to itself.14 A
person despairs when they refuse the burden of becoming who they are, when they collapse in the face
of possibility and shun the call towards authentic individuation . To exist as a human being is always
to stand between actuality and potentiality, never fully complete, always responsible for choosing
oneself. Despair is the refusal of this responsibility. It is easier, even comforting, to surrender identity
to external structures. Prison, as Shawshank demonstrates, offers precisely this comfort: a life without
choice, without risk, without inwardness. Interestingly, this predicament finds expression not only in
the pris on’s inmates, further emphasizing the universality of Kierkegaard’s concept.
To describe the ways in which individuals relate to this burden, Kierkegaard distinguishes
between three modes of existence: the aesthetic, the ethical, and the religious.15 The aesthetic life
avoids responsibility through distraction, pleasure, or routine , any way of escaping the need to choose
oneself. The ethical life accepts duty and social identity, but still grounds meaning in external norms
and recognition. Only the re ligious life embraces true inward freedom: the acceptance that no objective
assurance exists, that truth requires choice, and that one must commit without proof. Faith is therefore
not certainty, but risk. “Without risk,” Kierkegaard writes indeed , “no fai th.”16 This inward movement
culminates in what Kierkegaard calls the leap of faith. The leap is not irrational, but it is rather trans -
rational. It is the moment when the self chooses a path without the security of guarantees, acting “on
the strength of th e absurd” —the strength of something that cannot be justified by logic or evidence,
but must be affirmed inwardly.17 In Shawshank , this philosophical structure quickly becomes the

Incarceration as Existential Refuge
If anxiety is the dizziness of freedom, then the simplest way to silence anxiety would seemingly be to
eliminate freedom. The Shawshank Redemption reveals the paradox of incarceration: punishment can
become sanctuary. As Red explains, “first you hate these walls… then you get used to them… then
you depend on them.” In Kierkegaardian terms, the prison offers relief from the burden of possibility.
Before Andy, there are no choices to make, no futures to construct, no selves to become. The
architecture of the institution replaces inward existence with outward routine: where to walk, when to
eat, how to speak, when to sleep. Deleuze and Guattari describe such institutional ordering as an
example of striated space: regimented, partitioned, and governed by rules that determine movement
and inhabitance .18 Shawshank is emblematic of this striation, as every corridor, schedule, and gesture
is tightly structure d by authority. The chessboard motif makes this visual : Andy is introduced as
someone who “likes chess,” a clue that he understands how striated systems work and how they can
be navigated. Yet his quiet acts of resistance —building the library, teaching lit eracy, playing Mozart,
and chiseling the wall —create small incursions of what Deleuze and Guattari call smooth space: zones
of improvisation, creativity, and self -determined movement that are juxtaposed with, but also related
to striated space . The tunnel is the film’s ultimate line of flight, detouring through the prison’s material
grid. The final sho t then completes this spatial logic. Andy and Red stand at the Pacific , what the two

Beforehand, Shawshank epitomizes how s tructure swallows freedom, and with freedom, the

the self to stand before its ow n potentiality.19 To despair in this way is not necessarily to suffer
spectacularly, but more tragically to evade the responsibility of existence. The men do not merely
suffer physical incarceration and lose their liberty , but they lose the very requirement to choose it. The
prison makes life easier by eliminating the self. It is not the withdrawal of punishment that terrifies the
inmates, but the return of agency. Red describes how the architecture of Shawshank exerts a
psychological transformati on: initial resistance gradually gives way to acceptance, until the prisoners
depend on the very structures that confine them. Routine has thus become protection. The film makes
this idea spatially explicit. The prison functions as what Michel Foucault cal ls a heterotopia , a counter -
site that mirrors the structure of society while inverting its values.20 The world outside is chaotic, fast,
indifferent; the world inside is ordered, predictable, legible. The prison is therefore a refuge from
uncertainty more than a locale of spatial confinement .
The fact that Shawshank can feel psychologically safer than the outside world is the clearest
indication of despair. For Kierkegaard, this would not be the loss of the self, but the refusal of the self.
To Red in h is secular environment, it is being “institutionalized.” Jürgen Habermas describes late
modernity as “postsecular”: a cultural moment in which belief and unbelief coexist, and where
religious vocabulary resurfaces not as doctrine, but as a language for exp eriences that secular
rationality cannot dissolve.21 The nomenclature and disciplinary demarcation changes, but not the
human experience it tries to analyze. In line with this postsecular environment that film and audience

without internal form, without a self to return to. Shawshank therefore complicates conventional
narratives of liberation. In most prison films, escape marks triumph. Here, escape marks crisis. What
the men fear is not confinement, but possibility. If the prison is a cage, it is one that many of its
occupants cling to because it kills anxiety. And in killing anxiety, it also kills hope.

walls, barred cells, and regimented movement , it quickly reveals itself as something more complex.

nor merely as the symbolic or imagined space that exists in the minds of prisoners and authorities

The Despair of Weakness
Brooks Hatlen is the film’s most devastating illustration of what Kierkegaard calls the despair of
weakness : the inability to become oneself when confronted with the possibility pointed out above .
In The Sickness Unto Death , Kierkegaard writes that despair is not dramatic or spectacular , but quiet
and ordinary, the “refusal to be a self.”25 It occurs when the individual cannot bear the weight of
freedom and collapses into nonbeing. Brooks’s release from prison is not a victory —it is a death
sentence , as he himself senses when pondering the desperate me asure of harming an inmate . The world
outside the walls offers him what prison had removed: the dizzying openness of possibility , amplified

Inside Shawshank, Brooks is a librarian, a mentor, and a gentle authority. Notably, this role
extends across the various spaces discussed in pre vious sections, as Brooks has a role to serve in the
official order of inmates, as well as the small acts of deviation that continue to occur, as he smuggles

identity, routine, community, even affection. After his release, the world becomes intolerably bright
and fast. “I saw an automobile once when I was a boy, but now they’re everywhere,” he writes. The
letters tremble , and he cannot cross a street without f ear. He cannot sleep without the walls that once
confined him. His life has become possibility without structure, freedom without anchoring, anxiety
with no refuge. Without a place to be, he does not know how to be —as also for the audience, by
extension. In Kierkegaard ian terms, Brooks’s inward self has been replaced by the outward routines
of prison life. The institution carried his identity for him. He never had to choose himself, hope for
himself, or become himself. He inhabited Soja ’s Thirdspace : not merely the physical prison
(Firstspace) nor merely the imagined one (Secondspace), but a lived environment in which the walls
provided orientation, stability, and meaning. Shawshank may have physically confin ed Brooks , but it
also furnished him with a worl d. His new freedom destroys that world. This is why Brooks’s suicide
note is not bitter, but tragically apologetic. “I’ve decided not to stay. I doubt they’ll kick up any fuss,
not for an old crook like me.” He is not angry, resentful, or vengeful ––he also hasn’t relapsed into
crime. He is empty. A clinical description of the causative trajectory into depression can be the collapse
that follows unmanageably sustained anxiety when the nervous system can no longer bear possibility :
it shuts down all agency an d desire.26 Brooks’s death appears as an act of despairing passion as well
as a quiet surrender. He dies because he cannot inhabit the world as a self.

Crucially for our reading , the film makes clear that Brooks’s tragedy is not failure but human
vulnera bility. As the audience may identify, i n a fully secular world, there is no metaphysical rescue:
no miracle, no providence, no transcendent meaning that steps in to catch him. This is
Habermas’s postsecular condition in its starkest form. The prison eliminated anxiety for an entire five
decades, and the world immediately reactivated it. Without inward resources (imagination, hope,
spiritual or secular identity ) freedom becomes uninhabitable. Brooks , as this section has pointed out,
is not crushed by p unishment, but by possibility . His final words (“Maybe I should kill myself. I’m
tired of being afraid all the time” ) collapse Kierkegaard’s distinction between fear and anxiety. Fear
has objects. Anxiety does not. His last act, carving “Brooks was here,” attempts to give the world a
trace of his existence, a permanent mark that his self could not sustain. It is the aesthetic life’s final
gesture: the memorialization of a self that just could not continue. The Shawshank Redemption places
no judgment on him. Instead, it uses his death to illuminate what Andy will later survive: the need to
create an inward life strong enough to withstand freedom. Brooks dies because he cannot hold himself
together when the world demands that he be a self. His death is the fil m’s most heartbreaking
conclusion: punishment did not destroy him , but freedom did.
Andy Dufresne: Knight of Faith in the Immanent Frame
Where Brooks collapses under the weight of possibility, Andy Dufresne later survives by cultivating
an inward life strong enough to bear freedom. In Kierkegaardian terms, Andy embodies the religious
mode of existence : the individual who acts without certainty, sustains hope without proof, and commits
to a future that has absolutely no guarantee. Andy’s faith is no t grounded in the metaphysical assurance
that the warden carries with him as performative act but derives from an inexhaustible inward
discipline . His refusal to surrender imagination, memory, and possibility transforms his imprisonment
into a space of mea ning, as the confinement in the hole after the music scene underlines powerfully .
He teaches Tommy to read, builds the library letter by letter, and plays Mozart over the loudspeakers ,

Returning to Charles Taylor’s vocabulary, Andy is a fully secular saint: he lives entirely within
the immanent frame , a world without divine intervention, miracles, or providence , yet generates a form
of redemption that is recognizably spiritual.27 Radical Theology describes this as Christianity emptied
of metaphysical claims but alive as human action.28 Andy is never depicted praying for rescue , but he
creates it himself . He does not preach hope; he enacts it. His faith is always lived, not spoken. A subtle
but revealing detail reinforces this logic of inward redemption. Andy hides his rock hammer inside a
hollowed -out Bible , an object the warden clutches as a symbol of religious authority but never reads.
The warden’s faith is theatrical and punitive: he uses Scripture to justify violence, corruption, and
exploitation. Andy, by contrast, practices a postsecular Christianity grounded not in metaphysical
belief but in action. The Bible does not save him because it is holy : it literally saves him because it
conceals the tool of his freedom. Salvation lies within, both literally and figuratively. When, after
Andy’s escape, the warden opens the Bible and discovers the hammer inside, he finds a bookmark at
the beginning of Exodus , a book defined by liberation from captivity. More than anything, t he scene
exposes the difference between possessing a text and being transformed by it. The warden knows
Scripture by memory but not by meaning . Andy , conversely, enacts meaning without ever preaching
it. His freedom is the product of inward life, imagination, patience, and hope , ironically the very
qualities the prison was designed to extinguish. The warden thus believes in Scripture as text , but Andy
believes in Scripture as act. One impriso ns in God’s name , the other escapes by living what the text
means.

“swim through a river of shit,” as Red vividly puts it, and emerge clean. The scene indeed evokes
baptism, but stripped of metaphysics —as water does not cleanse his soul , but removes the filth of
institutional corruption. This is a rare moment of the sacramental stripped of the metaphysical in
cinematic history . When Andy bursts from the pipe and stands in the rain with his arms outstretched,
the image resembles a cruciform po sture, but the film refuses miracle. His rebirth is the result of work,
patience, and imagination , faith translated into action. His redemption is self-made, not given. Andy’s
escape therefore reveals the true meaning of hope in Shawshank as beyond optimis m and fantasy, but
as the courage to act without assurance. In a world where despair is easy and the burden of selfhood is
heavy, Andy survives by creating an inner life that no walls can contain. He becomes, in Kierkegaard’s
sense, a knight of faith in a secular world.
At the same time, the film codes Andy’s hope as more than self -belief or mere optimism.
Shawshank gives him a recognizably religious literacy (his ease with Scripture in the warden’s cell;
the Bible as a recurring object of interpretive s truggle) and frames hope as something with a kind of
objective durability —“good things… never die” —rather than a private preference. One can therefore
concede a subtle metaphysical horizon in Andy: a sense that reality is not finally chaotic, that there is
an order worth aligning oneself with, and that the human person is answerable to more than expediency.
The crucial point, however, is that the film refuses to stabilize this horizon as doctrine , precisely in

The Gospel According to Red
Andy is Shawshank’s knight of faith, and Red is its evangelist. His arc poetically embodies
Kierkegaard’s transformation from the ethical life as one defined by social expectations and external
validation to the religious life, which requires inward commitment without certainty. For most of the
film, Red is the rationalist, the skeptic, the man who knows how the world works. “Hope is a dangerous
thing, ” he warns Andy. “Hope can drive a man insane.” Hope, to Red, is a fantasy, a delusion, an
invitation to despair. Better to be realistic, keep one’s head down, and play by the rules. This is the
ethical self: the self as defined by roles and institutions , which Red identifies with more than any other
inmates we meet . His parole hearings make this visible to the audience . In each early hearing, Red
performs rehabilitation. He uses the vocabulary the system wants: alluding to remorse, repentance,
reformation. The audience also learns that this is a performative act, a guessing -game that rightly
recognizes the arbitrary interpretation of rehabilitation. Red here does not speak truth but strategy. And
every time, the board stamps DENIED. Kierkegaard calls this “the despair of defiance”: the self that
maintains control by refusing inwardness, living according to external expectations rather than inward
truth.31 Red lives ethically, which is a start, but not authentically.

Conclusion: Leaping Out of Heterotopi a’s Confinement
For most of the film, Shawshank functions as Foucault’s heterotopia , as a space set apart from society,
governed by its own logic, its own rituals, and its own forms of temporal experience. It is a refuge
from possibility as much as a site of punishment. Within its walls, the men are protected from the
dizzying openness of freedom; the heterotopia gives them structure where the world offers none.
Brooks dies because he cannot survive outside it. Red fears the same fate. For both, the prison is
paradoxically livable precise ly because it dissolves the need to choose oneself. Andy alone pushes
beyond this heterotopic frame. For him, Shawshank becomes not only Firstspace (material) nor
merely Secondspace (imagined), but a Thirdspace : a lived environment where inward possibility
reshapes material reality. His inner life , assuming external representation through music, memory,
learning, and the secret tunnel , compromises what the institution claims is solid and total. In
Kierkegaard’s vocabulary, Andy refuses despair. He refuses t o let the world define the limits of the
self. Where Brooks sees only walls, Andy sees another kind of deviation : an opening hidden within
the structure. The leap of faith begins long before the escape ; the prison breaks for Andy the moment
the wall does. This is why Zihuatanejo matters , even before it materializes . It begins as utopia in the
literal sense: no place , an imagined future with no basis in the present. Red justifiably dismisses it as
fantasy. But for Kierkegaard, possibility becomes real the moment the individual commits inwardly to
it. Acting “on the strength of the absurd,” Andy behaves as if Zihuatanejo already exists —saving
money, hiding documents, and building rout es. For once, u topia ceases to be dream and becomes task.

Zihuatanejo is no longer mere metaphor but geography. Notice that h ope did not rescue Andy; Andy

world to change but acts in the world to change it. Andy’s labor, patience, and imagination enact this
theology in material form .34 Zihuatanejo exists because he opens the confines of his world. Andy also
escapes not because the world opened for him, but because he opened the world. The journey from
Shawshank to the Pacific is therefore a movement from protected despair to dangerous f reedom, and
from the denial of possibility to its embrace. Brooks tragically could not inhabit that freedom. Red
learns to. Andy shows that utopia, like faith, is not given , it is made. This is the epitome of
Kierkegaard’s relation to one’s faith.
Shaws hank therefore becomes a postsecular Thirdspace: materially restrictive, psychologically
suffocating, yet shot through with invisible apertures of possibility . Andy survives by cultivating an
inner world and only when he discovers that deviation is structu rally possible, that the wall crumbles,
does the imagined space become a literal escape route. His freedom is made. In a place built to
eliminate possibility, he reveals that possibility was always there , a discovery the film discourages for
the majority o f its runtime . Andy’s survival is mental, imaginative, and inward. Every human being,
whether free or imprisoned, must spend a lifetime inside their own head. Shawshank suggests that the
true prison is not the Firstspace of stone walls, nor the Secondspace of routine and institutional identity,
but the collapse of the inner world , as Andy endures because his imagination remains unconfined. His

while never transcending the world. Its miracles are human: patience, imagination, loyalty, conviction ,
education . In the end, Shawshank crucially proposes that hope is not mere optimism but work , an

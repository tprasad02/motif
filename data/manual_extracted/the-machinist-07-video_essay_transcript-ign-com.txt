IGN Interviews Brad Anderson

And in the eye of the beholder, neither are the box office receipts. Now the thirty-nine-year-old Anderson has completed
The Machinist
, starring Batman-to-be Christian Bale. It's about a man named Trevor Reznik – a man who never sleeps. And he's haunted by a strange character, a menacing fellow machinist named Ivan (played by John Sharian). You can check out an exclusive clip from the film

IGN FilmForce's Steve Head spoke with Anderson about the making of
The Machinist
Q: This is no doubt the question most at the tip of everyone's tongue after they've seen a couple photos or they've seen
The Machinist
, so by impulse I'm starting with this question. Did you expect that Christian Bale was going to emaciate himself the way he did for this role? Did he have to go that far?
BRAD ANDERSON:
He had to lose the weight, but I didn't realize he ended up losing sixty-three pounds. We never talked about it. I didn't hire him because he said he would lose sixty-three pounds. It was apparent in the script. We both loved Scott [Kosar]'s script. And the character that he plays is described as kind of a walking skeleton. It
was
described as a walking skeleton. So, it was pretty implicit that whoever played the part was going to have to lose some weight.
Q: He seems like the kind of actor who would take a chance like this.
ANDERSON:
Christian expressed interest in it, and he wanted to do it, I also knew that as an actor he's the kind of guy that takes those kinds of chances. He's done it with his other movies. He sort of transformed himself physically, I think, when he played the character in
American Psycho
as well. I knew that he would do something, but I didn't expect him to lose sixty-three pounds. And I don't think he did either. He said he kind of pushed it maybe even beyond where he was intending to go. I just thought he'd lose a few pounds and then we'd put him in baggy clothes.
Christian Bale and Brad Anderson on the set of
The Machinist
Q: You shot the movie in Barcelona?
ANDERSON:
Yeah, we shot the movie in Barcelona, Spain.
Q: That's kind of a change of pace for a story that takes place in Los Angeles. But you know, it's a movie so I guess you can do it anywhere if it works.
ANDERSON:
As the movie goes, the location is like, yeah, where is it?
Q: I couldn't quite place it.
ANDERSON:
That's actually the point. For this movie, it's a weird reality.
Q: I though it was Southern California or Florida, but it's never stated. Overall it was kind of like nowhere.
ANDERSON:
That was sort of the intent; to be non-specific.
Q: Why'd you guys shoot it in Barcelona?
ANDERSON:
We ended up shooting it in there for a number of reasons. But primarily it was because we weren't able to cobble the money together to do it here in the States.
Q: Okay, but say for example, the scenes at Michael Ironside's [character's] house. Did you even do a little bit of filming here?
ANDERSON:
Nope, that was Spain.
Q: Pretty convincing. It was primarily a financial reason?
ANDERSON:
Yeah, what happened was the Spanish company Filmax approached us about shooting the film there. They loved the script and they wanted to work with me and they said, "We'll finance the movie. We'll give you five million to do it, but the condition is you've got to shoot it in Barcelona." I was like, "But the story's set in Los Angeles." Scott's original script was quite specific that it was Los Angeles. I was like, "How are we going to make Los Angeles in Barcelona?" That's a little hard.
Q: You did a preliminary scout though?
ANDERSON:
I went over there on a preliminary scout, yes, just to see if it was possible. The irony you realize, of course, is that whenever you travel these days you can always find a little bit of America. You know? [Laughs] Anywhere in the world.
Q: So you were pretty much in and around Barcelona?
ANDERSON:
Yeah, once you get outside the real center of Barcelona, and into the industrial zones, or whatever, it feels like it could be America. I mean, some parts just feel very generic. So I thought, I think maybe we can really pull this off.
Q: You found the right locations that convinced you that you could pull it off?
ANDERSON:
Not only that, once we realized that we weren't going to be able to make it look
specifically
like Los Angeles, we decided to consciously go for some kind of a generic American reality. I told the production designer, "It's America, but it's some kind of generic America." It's like the way you might imagine America if you have never been to America. So we didn't want to add all the cultural signifiers, like McDonald's or Starbucks, blah, blah, blah. We wanted it to be really bland and straightforward. Hence, we looked for locations that just were like generic. And we got them.
Q: With some of the shots there are things that can give it away that it's not LA.
ANDERSON:
You mean the metro. Right, LA doesn't have that kind of metro. [Laughs] But then again, what we were looking for is a surreal quality. The movie has a sort of surreal quality to it. For me, the ideal thing was to have the audience leave feeling just as, like,
dislocated
as Trevor Reznik, the character. To have them not know where they are or when they are. We didn't want to place anything in the story that even told you
when
it takes place. We wanted it to have a certain timeless feeling to it.
Q: As Reznik, Bale is sort of that disoriented in the way he carries himself. He slowly comes to these realizations. One of the things I was also curious about is how you paced the film; the ebb and flow of it. Did you abide by specific transitions and placement of scenes in the script, or did you change things around to reveal things at your speed? Because I think that what works for this movie is the mood.
ANDERSON:
I wasn't interested in making a thriller or a horror movie. In my mind, this movie is a slowly, unraveling nightmare. And I always feel like the scariest things happen kind of almost
slowly
. In some ways you could interpret the script as being a real straightforward thriller, kind of like
Fight Club
or something.
Q: In a way, it is kind of like
Fight Club
ANDERSON:
It has similar story elements, but again, that's by sheer coincidence. I'm just saying that it could have been a more straightforward thriller, but I wanted it to feel – I wanted the pacing to be more like the pacing would be if someone who could never sleep had to live through this. This movie is about a guy who can't sleep, and he's literally kind of like sleepwalking through his life. I wanted to have that pacing. That languid quality. And it's also a kind of throwback to an earlier kind of filmmaking. Our influences in this movie were more like Hitchcock than they were David Fincher. [Laughs]
Q: The score also seemed like it was Hitchcock-influenced, Bernard Herman. It has this subdued swaying and pulling. It draws you into watching it.
ANDERSON:
The score as well. The score was totally a throwback to all those Bernard Herman scores that he did for Hitchcock. Lush orchestrated scores. And you might have heard a style we associate with older horror movies and science fiction films. A lot of the music feels old school. A little old fashioned. It didn't want to have a hip contemporary score, because again, just to kind of fight some of the conventions. If you did want to call this movie a horror movie or a thriller, you could do that.
Q: I was going over it with a couple friends, and I wasn't sure what to call it actually.
ANDERSON:
Yeah, but I don't like to think of it as a horror movie, because horror movies bring to mind, like
The Texas Chainsaw Massacre
Nightmare on Elm Street
or movies that work to shock you. This, to me, is more of a movie about a slow build up of dread. It's not about shocking you with some gore. I mean, there are
moments
like that, but this is more about this guy's character. It's really told so much from his point of view.
Q: I'm inhibited about asking a couple questions that in some way might merely refer to what happens at the end of the movie. But I think I'm safe with this one; keeping it ambiguous. Did Reznik in his past ever really know someone like Ivan?
ANDERSON:
I'll put it this way, Reznik hangs out with him and Ivan is in some ways trying to lead him in the right direction. He's sort of trying to goad him into remembering. You can't run away from something because it will catch up with you.
Q: And it's gruesome in some parts, but
The Machinist
isn't about that.
ANDERSON:
That's why I think the horror film tag on this movie is a little misleading. There's elements of it, but I would say this film is more in my mind of a movie that shares certain things with great literature. Not that I'm comparing it to Dostoyevsky or Kafka, but those are two writers whose themes often dealt with guilty consciences.
Q: If I recall, I think there's a moment in the film where he quotes Kafka.
ANDERSON:
Yeah, there are a couple little moments where, like, when he's reading Dostoyevsky's
The Idiot
. And there's certainly a Kafkaesque quality to the story. I mean, this guy is on this crazy goose chase and he has to find the answer to the puzzle. He's tormenting himself. It's not like some grand conspiracy that's out to get him. He fuels his own paranoia.
Q: What was it that appealed to you most about making this movie?
ANDERSON:
There are elements that will appeal to people who like horror films, but in making this movie the things that appealed to me about it were theses other themes and the character exploration. We tried to make him a character that you could sympathize with. I mean, why is he torturing himself? What is wrong with this guy? He looks like he's dying.
Q: I must say, the fella who plays Ivan...
ANDERSON:
John Sharian.
Q: ...he must have given a really good audition. He looks like he could.
ANDERSON:
Well, actually, he really did. [Laughs] I thought he was outstanding. That's how I chose him. John Sharian is his name; he's an American actor who lives in London. He's done mostly theater. I was drawn to him mostly because he just did a really good audition, but also because I just… [Laughs] there's just something really perverse about that fact that he kind of looks like a young Marlon Brando in
Apocalypse Now
Q: Funny you should mention that. That's what I was thinking. His profile. He
does
look like Brando. That totally jumped into my mind when I was watching the movie. What kind of delivery was he going for? [Laughs]
ANDERSON:
What was he going for... it sounds like he's got some sort of Louisiana, some sort of Southern reality. You know, he was playing the sort of quintessential sleazy guy you meet a bar. [Laughs] And suddenly he's your best friend. It's really kind of a metaphor. It could have been anything, so we wanted to create kind of character was highly memorable and weird. A villain, but also a sleazy charmkin as well.
Q: How have the audience's reactions to the movie struck you so far? I mean, how satisfied are you with the reactions and what you've been hearing?
ANDERSON:
There's been good reactions to the film. I think people are really intrigued by it. We've screened it at a number of film festivals. We premiered it at Sundance back in January, and then we've been to other places. I don't know how the movie is going to perform. You never know how a movie is going to perform. I think the reviews that we've got so far really have been quite good. But, at the end of the day, you never know. It's all a crap shoot.
John Sharian as Ivan in
The Machinist
Q: Paramount is certainly behind you on this. Hopefully they'll give the movie some consistent attention; you know, make sure it can stay in theaters for a while.
ANDERSON:
Yes, they were very supportive. And the movie's opening October 22nd in limited release to start, New York and LA; then it'll expand afterwards. I think it opens in Boston on November 12th. But you know, these are the kind of films that need to build up by word of mouth. It's not like the movie is going to do boffo business its first weekend. It's too small of a film.
Q: For one of these I'm thinking it's a matter of exposure. It's not going to explode on the scene, but it just has to be available because, like, eventually people will hear of it and want to see it.
ANDERSON:
Yeah, and you know there are good films that just never see the light of day because they're pushed out by another big movie that opens that weekend. Or for whatever reason they just don't get the kind of attention they deserve. And on the opposite, there are films that are utter crap that end up doing incredibly well. As a filmmaker you just make the movie you want to make and you sort of put your faith in the distributor to make sure the right people see it. And to keep it in the theaters long enough so that it builds an audience.
Q: I'd heard some people say
The Machinist
could maybe stay around the way
Memento
did.
ANDERSON:
The thing about a film like
Memento
, which is not un-similar to this in size and all that, is that that film started in two theaters and over the course of a couple months people started talking and it became a success over time. That's because the distributors were willing to keep it in the theaters. The problem nowadays is that there's so much competition. There's so many movies that a distributor will, like, pull a movie after a week and a half if it doesn't seem to be doing well. They're screwed, because these movies don't immediately succeed. They take time to build up an audience.
Q: That kind of happened with
Session 9
, your last movie. Was it as "out there" as you would have liked it to be?
ANDERSON:
Yeah, but USA Films didn't know what to do with the film.
Q: Not that
Session 9
didn't work out in theaters as well as everyone thought it would. But it does well on DVD.
ANDERSON:
In the end it did. I mean, like for
The Machinist
I want it to get out there and do well because I love it. But my other films have all been smaller movies, and at the end of the day
Session 9
just didn't have the support it needed from the distributor, like I said. But it has gone on to do very well on DVD and it did very well overseas. So, that's another reason I shot
The Machinist
in Spain, because
Session 9
did
really
well in Spain of all places.
Q: A change of subject. How do you find a car like that in Spain?
ANDERSON:
The Firebird? [Laughs] Search vigorously. It was one of two red Firebirds in all of Spain [Laughs] that we were able to track down. That actually was probably the
biggest
hassle in making this film was trying to find American cars. The moment you see a Renault or a Citron drive by you know you're not in America.
Q: You really orchestrated the traffic sequences. Your camera moves
down
through the frame. Were those effects, the cars coming through the intersections?
ANDERSON:
No, they were actually that close. All stunts. Yeah, but getting all those American cars was a drag. To find a Firebird. Oh man. But to find a Chevy pickup in Spain? There are no pickup trucks there. [Laughs]
Q: What did you do?
ANDERSON:
What we did was we went to an American military base where they had a lot of old vehicles. You know, because they'd always import American cars and use American cars there. We used a lot of those.
Q: And that's how you found the Firebird?
ANDERSON:
No, actually that was a car collector. He let us use it, which was I guess kind of stupid because we ended up trashing the thing. [Laughs] He didn't realize what we were going to do with it.
Q: And the carnival?
ANDERSON:
Just outside Barcelona is a little amusement park. And we were able shoot there. There are certain places where you can almost make it work. You can build an American reality.

ÓÄÇe Truman Show predicts the rise of Big Brother (the TVshow), with constant surveillance acting as a form of entertainment for the masses. Dark City is more overtly science-fictional, set in anenvironment mimicking film noir but run by aliens. ÓÄÇis paper looks at how thesetwo films depict the surveillance of the (sub)urban environment as a way of manipulating and shaping its inhabitants, and how these depictionsparallel society at the end of the 20 Century.In 1998, two American films were released that question how identity is partially formed by one‚Äôs environment, ÓÄÇe Truman Show(Weir 1998) and Dark City (Proyas 1998). ÓÄÇe Truman Show is more familiar to non-specialist audiences, a satire about a then-nascent genre of reality television, which, rather than using the format of a house filled with cameras, depicts an entire town filmedfor television, inhabited by actors except for the star of the show ‚Äì Truman Burbank ‚Äì who is unaware that his entire life has beenbroadcast. ÓÄÇe Truman Show also relies on a comedic version of 1950s melodrama to give the audience an idea of the genre they arewatching. ÓÄÇe environment of this world presented on screen ‚Äì old fashioned cars, buses and movie theatres ‚Äì suggests anyperiod between 1930 and 1950 (Figure 1).
